  • At the recent exhibition held at "BUG ART SWEATER" in Tokyo, artists with color blindness and visually impaired artists collaborated in a highly challenging and inspiring art practice focused around "visual impairment." I believe this exhibition not only disrupted our conventional understanding of "seeing" and "art," but also expanded the perceptual boundaries of what art fundamentally can be.

    In the exhibition, viewers were permitted, with artists’ consent, to touch the artworks. The textures, whether raised or recessed, rough or smooth, and the distinct edges of the materials collectively formed a non-visual painting experience. For many visitors, this tactile interaction was unfamiliar yet profound, representing a re-learning of how one can "see."

    We often perceive painting exclusively as a visual art form. In today's highly visual and digital image-driven society, "vision" seems to be the only gateway to art. However, this assumption has, from the very beginning, excluded a significant group of people. The creative practices and experiences of blind and color-blind individuals remind us that art goes beyond the mere perception of color and form—it can equally be expressed through touch, sound, or even the reconstruction of time and memory.

    Additionally, the exhibition highlights society's increasing attention towards bodily differences and sensory diversity. Art is shifting away from presuming able-bodiedness as the default, moving instead towards greater openness and inclusivity. In this exhibition, "disability" is no longer perceived as a limitation; rather, it emerges as a unique medium—a new way to engage with and understand the world.
